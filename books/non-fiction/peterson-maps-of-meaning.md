| | |
|---|---|

## Synopsis

*Maps of Meaning: The Architecture of Belief* is Jordan Peterson's attempt to explain
why people are willing to fight and die over belief systems, and what myths reveal
about the structure of human experience. Drawing on psychology, neuroscience,
mythology, and religious narrative, Peterson argues that the world is encountered
not as a place of objective facts but as a forum for action — a landscape of the
known (order), the unknown (chaos), and the heroic process that mediates between
them. He reads recurring mythological motifs as encoded maps for confronting chaos
and renewing order, and links the failure of meaning to totalitarianism and
atrocity. Dense and interdisciplinary, it lays the theoretical groundwork for his
later, more popular writing.
