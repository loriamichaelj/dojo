# The Wealth of Nations

| | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Adam Smith |
| **Published** | 1776 |

## Synopsis

*An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* is the founding
work of modern economics. Adam Smith argues that national prosperity arises not
from hoarded gold but from productive labor, and that the division of labor —
specialization — is the primary engine of productivity growth. He introduces the
"invisible hand": individuals pursuing their own self-interest in competitive
markets are led, unintentionally, to promote the common good through the
price mechanism. The book develops theories of value, wages, profit, and rent,
mounts a sustained critique of mercantilism, and makes the case for free trade and
limited but purposeful government. Its ideas about markets, incentives, and
specialization remain foundational to economic and quantitative finance thinking.
